Motivations of External Migration Per Country

As seen in the above chart, economic insecurity is the leading cause of migration (source: UN World Food Programme, April 2021 survey of Northern Triangle inhabitants). Despite the financial and emotional costs associated with migrating from the Northern Triangle (El Salvador, Honduras, and Guatemala) to the United States, indviduals take on this burden every year in search of better salaries, working conditions, and opportunities to send money home.

Migrants who end up working in the destination country long enough are able to offset the cost of their migration and send remittances home. They also spend money in the United States (for food, housing, and other services), invigorating the economy.

Given the risks inherent in migration and the possibility of being sent back, is the cost worth it? At what point do working migrants offset that cost? How much are they contributing to the US economy depending on what jobs are available? First, we need to understand what their earning potential is based on the roles they are able to hold in the destination country.

Original Occupation

US Occupation

The above sankey chart represents the occupations for migrants before and after their migration to the US. There are 8 different categories of occupations. Before migration, across all the categories, the agricultural sector has the most workers, with 392. The "student" and "informal work" categories also have over 300 people each.

After migration, many of the migrants change jobs. By hovering over the origin country occupation nodes, we get perspective regarding changes for people for a particular occupation when they move to the US. Hovering over the US occupation nodes, we can see how people from different occupations in the origin countries take up jobs in a new occupation category. For example, 458 migrants in the US do "informal work." This group includes many who did informal work before, but also former farmers, students, and business owners, among others.

In reviewing this data, we wanted to connect more deeply with the people behind the numbers. We are highlighting here 10 USA-bound migrants who form a representative sample of the larger group.

Put another way, if this group of migrants were 10 people, who would they be? What would they be like? How can they help us better understand the tradeoff between the cost of migration, remittances, and money entering the USA on a more accessible scale? Move your mouse over each individual to learn more.

Consider the 26-year-old man from Guatemala. He spent $1516 USD to migrate and is currently living in the US. He has a salaried job in the US; assume he makes $800/month. He also sends home $200 USD as remittances every month. With these numbers, he will break even in a month, earning beyond the initial cost of migration. Every month forward, he will contribute $600 USD ($800-$200) to the United States economy.

Now, let's look at migration cost balance over time for these 10 migrants based on their initial migration cost, monthly remittances sent home, and earning potential.

Breakeven-Time (in months) on Migration Investment

As seen in the above graph, the time it takes to pay off the cost of the migration depends on the job secured by the migrant in the destination country and the amount of money they send back as remittances every month. It can take as little as 1 month to work off the cost of the migration and begin contributing to the economy, but for more expensive migrations, the payback period can be as long as a year.

The orange bars represent two migrants who paid thousands of US dollars to migrate but were sent back to their country of origin. They never had a chance to pay off the amount they invested in the migration. They never had the opportunity to work in the US, contribute to its economy, and send money back home.
